## [P_E1] — Equilibrium Existence: Economic Interpretation

**Proposition (restated in plain English):**
在给定紧致可行集合和连续收益条件下，企业可以选择一个利润最大化的质量—价格组合，消费者随后按净效用是否非负决定购买。

### The Core Mechanism

当企业能够承诺质量和价格时，它会预见消费者的购买 cutoff，并在可行质量—价格集合中选择利润最高的组合。消费者只需比较购买带来的净效用和不购买的外部选项。这个结构使模型形成一个清晰的顺序均衡：企业先选择，消费者后反应。

**Step-by-step mechanism:**
企业承诺 \((q,p)\) → 消费者观察并按 cutoff 购买 → 企业面对诱导需求最大化利润 → SPE 存在。

### The Intuition

The key insight is that the model is not a simultaneous bargaining problem; it is a firm commitment problem followed by consumer participation. Once the consumer reaction is pinned down by a cutoff, the firm's problem becomes a standard optimization problem.

**Why it would have been non-obvious before the model:**
Without specifying commitment, observability, and type distribution, it would be unclear whether quality upgrading is an equilibrium object or simply an exogenous change.

### What This Result Rules Out

It rules out the concern that the model has no equilibrium object to analyze, provided the compactness and continuity conditions are maintained.

### Real-World Counterpart

This is consistent with stylized markets where a firm announces a product version and price before consumers decide whether to buy. The result should not be read as claiming real firms always optimize this way; it is a theoretical benchmark.

### Policy / Welfare Interpretation

**Who benefits:** Not determined by existence alone.

**Who loses:** Not determined by existence alone.

**Distributional consequence:** Existence only establishes that later welfare comparisons are well-defined.

**Policy implication:** None directly.

### "So What?" — Introduction Paragraph

Before asking whether quality upgrading helps consumers, the paper first needs a disciplined object of analysis. The existence result supplies that object: a quality-price equilibrium with consumer participation. This allows the paper to ask how changes in quality move prices, participation, and welfare within a coherent framework.

### Connection to Prior Literature

This result inherits the standard logic of monopoly quality choice and vertical differentiation. It does not claim novelty; it supports the later welfare and comparative-static results.

### Limitations of the Interpretation

This interpretation should NOT be taken to imply:
- Equilibrium is unique without additional strict quasi-concavity.
- All real markets have compact quality and price domains.
- Consumers always observe quality perfectly.

The model abstracts away from competition, learning, search, and dynamic commitment problems.

## [P_U1] — Conditional Uniqueness: Economic Interpretation

**Proposition (restated in plain English):**
If the firm's profit problem has a single peak, then the equilibrium quality and price are unique.

### The Core Mechanism

When profit is strictly quasi-concave, any movement away from the unique peak lowers profit. Consumers may be indifferent exactly at the cutoff, but that type has zero mass under a continuous distribution. Therefore the economically relevant quality-price outcome is unique.

**Step-by-step mechanism:**
Strict quasi-concavity → single firm optimum → unique \((q,p)\) → cutoff indifference only measure-zero.

### The Intuition

The result reflects the fact that multiplicity here is not generated by strategic feedback among many firms. It can arise only from a flat or multi-peaked profit function.

**Why it would have been non-obvious before the model:**
Quality and price interact through demand; without a curvature condition, the profit landscape could have multiple local or global optima.

### What This Result Rules Out

It rules out equilibrium multiplicity only under the additional strict quasi-concavity condition.

### Real-World Counterpart

The model suggests that if a firm's quality-pricing problem has a stable single best product positioning, then comparative statics can focus on one equilibrium path. This is a modeling convenience, not a universal fact.

### Policy / Welfare Interpretation

No direct welfare implication; uniqueness mainly helps interpretation and comparative statics.

### "So What?" — Introduction Paragraph

A unique quality-price outcome makes the welfare effects of quality upgrading easier to interpret. When the firm has a single best response, changes in costs or quality technology move the market along one path rather than among many possible equilibria.

### Connection to Prior Literature

The result is a standard optimization implication, not a substantive departure from prior monopoly models.

### Limitations of the Interpretation

This interpretation should NOT be taken to imply:
- The primitives automatically guarantee strict quasi-concavity.
- Multiple product strategies cannot arise in real markets.
- Boundary optima are economically unimportant.

## [P_C1] — Individual Gain Threshold: Economic Interpretation

**Proposition (restated in plain English):**
Among consumers who keep buying after a quality upgrade, those with sufficiently high quality valuation gain, while those with low enough quality valuation lose if the price increase is large relative to the quality improvement.

### The Core Mechanism

When quality rises, every continuing buyer receives a gross quality benefit proportional to their type. But every continuing buyer also pays the same price increase. High-type consumers value the quality gain enough to offset the price increase; low-type consumers do not.

**Step-by-step mechanism:**
Quality upgrade → type-scaled quality benefit + common price increase → threshold type separates gainers from losers.

### The Intuition

The key insight is that a uniform price increase is not uniform in welfare terms. A high valuation consumer buys more quality per dollar; a low valuation consumer pays the same dollar increase for a smaller subjective gain.

**Why it would have been non-obvious before the model:**
Informal discussion often treats “better quality” as a uniform improvement. The model shows that the same objective improvement maps into different net utility changes across types.

### What This Result Rules Out

It rules out the claim that all continuing buyers must benefit from quality upgrading whenever the product is objectively better.

### Real-World Counterpart

This suggests a way to think about premiumization: some consumers may welcome higher product standards or better features, while others may experience the same change mainly as a price increase.

**Concrete illustrative example:**
If quality utility rises by 2 units and price rises by 6, then \(\theta^G=3\). A type \(\theta=4\) gains \(8-6=2\), while a type \(\theta=2\) loses \(4-6=-2\).

### Policy / Welfare Interpretation

**Who benefits:** High-valuation continuing buyers.

**Who loses:** Low-valuation continuing buyers when price pass-through is high enough.

**Distributional consequence:** Quality upgrading may redistribute surplus toward consumers who value quality most.

**Policy implication:** If a policy mandates quality upgrading, distributional effects should be evaluated, not just average quality.

### "So What?" — Introduction Paragraph

A product can become objectively better while some of its continuing buyers become worse off. The reason is simple but important: quality gains scale with willingness to pay, while price increases apply to everyone who keeps buying. This creates a distributional threshold inside the consumer population.

### Connection to Prior Literature

This refines standard vertical-quality intuition by expressing the consumer-side gain/loss cutoff explicitly. It is not a standalone novelty claim; it supports the paper's decomposition.

### Limitations of the Interpretation

This interpretation should NOT be taken to imply:
- All low-type consumers exit; some may continue buying and lose.
- Price increases are always caused by quality upgrading.
- The threshold applies when price enters utility nonlinearly or budgets bind.

## [P_C2] — Consumer-Surplus Threshold: Economic Interpretation

**Proposition (restated in plain English):**
In an interior market-coverage region, a marginal quality upgrade raises consumer surplus exactly when the average quality valuation of current buyers times the marginal quality improvement exceeds the price pass-through.

### The Core Mechanism

When quality increases slightly, current buyers receive extra value proportional to their average quality valuation. At the same time, they pay the marginal price increase. The marginal buyer's boundary movement does not directly add a first-order term because the marginal buyer receives zero net surplus at the cutoff.

**Step-by-step mechanism:**
Interior cutoff → marginal quality benefit to current buyers → price pass-through cost → consumer-surplus sign determined by average buyer valuation vs. pass-through.

### The Intuition

The key insight is that aggregate consumer surplus depends on the average valuation among buyers, not on the marginal buyer alone. The marginal buyer determines participation, but inframarginal buyers determine much of the surplus effect.

**Why it would have been non-obvious before the model:**
One might expect the marginal consumer to determine all welfare effects because the cutoff defines demand. The model shows that the cutoff's first-order boundary term vanishes, leaving average buyer valuation as the key object.

### What This Result Rules Out

It rules out evaluating quality upgrading solely by asking whether the marginal consumer is willing to buy. The inframarginal consumer base matters for welfare.

### Real-World Counterpart

This suggests that quality upgrades are more likely to raise consumer surplus when the existing buyer base places high value on quality and price pass-through is limited. It is a stylized mechanism, not an empirical claim about any named industry.

**Concrete illustrative example:**
If current buyers have average \(\theta=5\), marginal quality utility is 1, and pass-through is 3, consumer surplus rises. If pass-through is 7, consumer surplus falls.

### Policy / Welfare Interpretation

**Who benefits:** Current buyers as a group when their average marginal quality valuation exceeds price pass-through.

**Who loses:** Current buyers as a group when price pass-through dominates.

**Distributional consequence:** Aggregate consumer surplus can rise even while some low-type buyers lose, or fall even while high-type buyers gain.

**Policy implication:** The relevant statistic is not quality alone but quality benefit relative to price pass-through and buyer composition.

### "So What?" — Introduction Paragraph

The model identifies a simple welfare test for marginal quality upgrading. Higher quality benefits consumers only if the value current buyers place on that quality exceeds the price increase they face. This shifts attention from quality levels alone to the joint movement of quality, prices, and the composition of buyers.

### Connection to Prior Literature

This complements vertical-quality and price-discrimination models by isolating a consumer-surplus threshold. It refines rather than overturns existing theory.

### Limitations of the Interpretation

This interpretation should NOT be taken to imply:
- The formula applies under full coverage or zero coverage without modification.
- The price schedule \(p(q)\) is automatically differentiable when it comes from firm reoptimization.
- The result covers multidimensional quality or income effects.

## [P_W1] — Firm-vs-Planner Marginal Quality Wedge: Economic Interpretation

**Proposition (restated in plain English):**
For a fixed served consumer group, the firm values a marginal quality increase according to the marginal buyer, while a total-surplus planner values it according to the average served buyer; therefore the planner's marginal value of quality exceeds the firm's when served consumers are heterogeneous.

### The Core Mechanism

The firm uses price to extract willingness to pay at the participation margin. A small increase in quality raises willingness to pay for all served consumers, but the firm's pricing logic is disciplined by the marginal buyer. The planner, by contrast, counts the quality gains of every served consumer.

**Step-by-step mechanism:**
Fixed served group → firm prices at marginal buyer → planner counts all served buyers → average served valuation exceeds marginal valuation → marginal quality wedge.

### The Intuition

The result reflects a basic distinction between marginal revenue logic and social surplus logic. The firm asks how quality helps it sell profitably at the margin; the planner asks how quality affects all buyers who consume.

**Why it would have been non-obvious before the model:**
Quality upgrades are often discussed as cost increases or feature improvements. The model shows that the identity of the consumer whose valuation is internalized differs across private and social objectives.

### What This Result Rules Out

It rules out the idea that a monopolist's quality choice automatically reflects the social value of quality to all buyers.

### Real-World Counterpart

This suggests that private quality incentives may underweight benefits to inframarginal consumers. The result is stylized and applies as a marginal wedge under fixed coverage, not as an unconditional global ranking of firm and planner quality.

### Policy / Welfare Interpretation

**Who benefits:** Inframarginal consumers whose quality benefits are not fully internalized by the firm.

**Who loses:** Potentially consumers if the firm underprovides quality relative to the fixed-coverage planner benchmark.

**Distributional consequence:** The wedge arises because the firm focuses on the marginal participant while the planner aggregates gains across served consumers.

**Policy implication:** Policies that subsidize quality or set standards may be welfare-improving in some environments, but the model does not prove a general policy rule.

### "So What?" — Introduction Paragraph

Private firms and social planners may value quality improvements for different reasons. A firm disciplines quality choice through the marginal buyer, while a planner counts the gains to all buyers who receive the higher quality. This creates a marginal wedge that can make private quality incentives diverge from social welfare.

### Connection to Prior Literature

This interpretation complements monopoly quality choice and vertical differentiation by emphasizing the marginal-vs-average served consumer distinction.

### Limitations of the Interpretation

This interpretation should NOT be taken to imply:
- A global result that firm quality is always below planner quality.
- The wedge applies when coverage changes endogenously without further conditions.
- The result survives arbitrary nonconcavity without modification.

## [P_W2] — Distributional Welfare Reversal: Economic Interpretation

**Proposition (restated in plain English):**
Under an explicit pass-through comparison, quality can rise while some low-valuation continuing buyers lose and aggregate consumer surplus falls.

### The Core Mechanism

Quality upgrading raises gross value for all continuing buyers, but price pass-through subtracts a common amount from their net utility. If pass-through is high enough, low-valuation consumers lose even though the product is better. If the losses of low and marginal consumers outweigh gains to high-valuation consumers, total consumer surplus falls.

**Step-by-step mechanism:**
Quality rises → gross utility rises for all continuing buyers → price rises enough → low types lose / some exit → aggregate consumer surplus can fall.

### The Intuition

The key insight is that objective product improvement and consumer welfare improvement are different objects. Welfare depends on net surplus, not engineering quality.

**Why it would have been non-obvious before the model:**
Without separating gross utility, price, and participation effects, it is easy to mistake higher quality for higher consumer welfare.

### What This Result Rules Out

It rules out the unconditional claim that quality upgrading necessarily improves consumers' net welfare.

### Real-World Counterpart

This may illuminate premiumization or quality-standard changes where improved products become less attractive or less accessible to low-valuation consumers. This is a stylized possibility, not an empirical statement about any particular market.

**Concrete illustrative example:**
If a quality upgrade gives high types value 10 and low types value 3, but raises price by 5, high types gain while low types lose. If many consumers are low valuation, aggregate consumer surplus can fall.

### Policy / Welfare Interpretation

**Who benefits:** High quality-valuation consumers who continue buying.

**Who loses:** Low valuation continuing buyers and consumers who exit after the price increase.

**Distributional consequence:** Quality upgrading can be regressive in quality-valuation space: it favors consumers with high willingness to pay for quality.

**Policy implication:** Quality mandates or firm-led upgrades may require complementary access policies if policymakers care about low-valuation or price-sensitive consumers.

### "So What?" — Introduction Paragraph

The paper shows why a market can deliver better products without making consumers better off on average. If quality improvements are accompanied by sufficiently high price pass-through, the gains accrue mainly to consumers with high valuations for quality, while lower-valuation consumers lose surplus or exit. Quality upgrading is therefore a distributional event, not merely a technical improvement.

### Connection to Prior Literature

This complements vertical-quality models by organizing the consumer-welfare reversal through explicit pass-through and participation channels.

### Limitations of the Interpretation

This interpretation should NOT be taken to imply:
- The reversal occurs for every equilibrium-reoptimized price path.
- Quality upgrading is generally harmful.
- The model includes income constraints, multidimensional quality, or competition.

## [P_M1] — Quality-Utility Decomposition: Economic Interpretation

**Proposition (restated in plain English):**
The consumer-surplus effect of quality upgrading can be decomposed into gains to continuing buyers, price losses to continuing buyers, and the surplus effects of entry and exit.

### The Core Mechanism

A quality upgrade changes not only how much current buyers value the product, but also who buys. Continuing buyers experience a direct net utility change, entrants gain new surplus, and exiters lose old surplus. Aggregate consumer surplus is the sum of these channels.

**Step-by-step mechanism:**
Quality-price regime changes → purchase sets change → continuing/entrant/exiter decomposition → total CS change.

### The Intuition

The key insight is that quality upgrading changes both intensive welfare and extensive participation. Ignoring entry and exit misses part of the welfare effect.

**Why it would have been non-obvious before the model:**
A simple before/after quality comparison hides the composition change in the buyer pool.

### What This Result Rules Out

It rules out a welfare analysis that considers only consumers who buy in both regimes.

### Real-World Counterpart

The decomposition is useful for thinking about markets where upgrades both improve the product and change affordability. It is an accounting framework, not a direct empirical claim.

### Policy / Welfare Interpretation

**Who benefits:** Continuing buyers with high valuation and entrants who gain positive surplus.

**Who loses:** Continuing buyers with low valuation and exiters.

**Distributional consequence:** Aggregate welfare can mask offsetting movements across these groups.

**Policy implication:** Evaluations of quality policy should separate intensive gains from extensive participation changes.

### "So What?" — Introduction Paragraph

The model's decomposition clarifies why quality upgrading has ambiguous consumer effects. The same change can create gains for high-valuation buyers, losses for low-valuation buyers, and exclusion of marginal buyers. A useful welfare analysis must track all three channels.

### Connection to Prior Literature

This is best viewed as an organizing decomposition built from standard consumer-surplus logic.

### Limitations of the Interpretation

This interpretation should NOT be taken to imply:
- The decomposition itself is a new behavioral theorem.
- It determines the sign of welfare without further assumptions.
- It applies unchanged under nonlinear income effects.

## [P_B1] — Boundary Cases: Economic Interpretation

**Proposition (restated in plain English):**
If consumers are homogeneous, quality upgrading creates no distributional split among types; if price pass-through becomes very high relative to quality gains, the set of continuing buyers who gain shrinks toward the highest-valuation consumers.

### The Core Mechanism

Distributional conflict requires heterogeneity. When all consumers have the same valuation, they all experience the same net utility change. When pass-through is high, only the highest-valuation consumers can justify paying the higher price.

**Step-by-step mechanism:**
No heterogeneity → no type split; high pass-through → high gain threshold → few gainers among continuing buyers.

### The Intuition

The result clarifies which assumption drives the distributional story: heterogeneity in quality valuation. Without it, quality upgrading may still help or hurt consumers, but it does not sort them into gainers and losers by type.

**Why it would have been non-obvious before the model:**
The model separates average welfare effects from distributional effects.

### What This Result Rules Out

It rules out attributing distributional conflict to quality upgrading alone; the conflict requires heterogeneity and price movement.

### Real-World Counterpart

This suggests that quality upgrading is more distributionally consequential in markets where consumers differ substantially in how much they value quality. This is a stylized implication.

### Policy / Welfare Interpretation

**Who benefits:** Under high pass-through, only the highest quality-valuation continuing buyers.

**Who loses:** Lower valuation continuing buyers or consumers who exit.

**Distributional consequence:** Heterogeneity determines whether quality upgrading is broadly shared or concentrated.

**Policy implication:** Distributional analysis matters most when consumer valuations are dispersed.

### "So What?" — Introduction Paragraph

The model shows that heterogeneity is not a technical detail; it is the source of the distributional stakes. If consumers value quality similarly, quality upgrading is mostly an average welfare question. If valuations differ sharply, the same upgrade can divide consumers into winners, losers, and exiters.

### Connection to Prior Literature

This boundary result supports the paper's use of heterogeneous consumers and vertical quality.

### Limitations of the Interpretation

This interpretation should NOT be taken to imply:
- There is always a positive mass of continuing buyers.
- The result holds with atoms at the top type without modification.
- Exit and continuing-buyer loss are the same channel.

## Cross-Proposition Narrative

The paper's central message is that product quality upgrading is not a one-channel welfare event. Higher quality raises gross utility, but equilibrium prices and participation determine whether consumers actually gain. The model separates these channels and shows that consumer welfare depends on buyer composition, price pass-through, and the distinction between marginal and inframarginal consumers.

## Recommended Discussion Section Topics

1. **Interior versus boundary market coverage** — The main consumer-surplus threshold applies cleanly only in interior coverage; boundary cases require separate treatment.
2. **Marginal wedge versus global quality ranking** — The firm-vs-planner result should be presented as a marginal wedge unless stronger curvature conditions are added.
3. **Equilibrium price pass-through** — The welfare reversal is strongest as a pass-through comparison; future work should derive it from fully reoptimized firm pricing.
4. **Extensions to competition and quality uncertainty** — Oligopoly, multidimensional quality, and unobservable quality may change the welfare conclusions.
