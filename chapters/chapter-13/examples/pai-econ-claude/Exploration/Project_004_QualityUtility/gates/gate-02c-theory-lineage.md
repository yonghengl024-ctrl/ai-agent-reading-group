## THEORY LINEAGE STATEMENT

```text
THEORY LINEAGE STATEMENT
========================
Closest canonical ancestor:
  Mussa and Rosen (1978), “Monopoly and product quality,” Journal of Economic Theory 18(2): 301–317, DOI 10.1016/0022-0531(78)90085-6.
  This citation was verified in the current session via Crossref API metadata for title, authors, year, journal, volume, issue, pages, and DOI.

What is inherited from the ancestor:
  1. A monopolist or firm with market power chooses product quality and price.
  2. Consumers differ by a one-dimensional type θ that governs willingness to pay for quality.
  3. Quality is vertically ranked: higher θ consumers value quality more strongly.
  4. Consumers participate only if net utility from purchase is at least the outside option.
  5. Firm-chosen quality and price jointly determine market coverage and surplus allocation.

What is changed from the ancestor:
  1. The baseline begins with a single quality-price pair rather than a full menu of quality versions, to isolate the welfare effect of “quality upgrading.”
  2. The model’s central object is not only the firm’s optimal product line, but the decomposition of consumer utility changes after quality upgrading.
  3. The paper explicitly tracks distributional welfare across consumer types: gainers, losers, entrants, and exiters.

What new economic mechanism is added:
  Quality upgrading changes consumer welfare through three linked channels. First, a higher quality q raises gross utility θv(q), with larger gains for higher θ. Second, because higher quality raises cost or changes the firm’s optimal markup, the equilibrium price p changes, subtracting from every buyer’s net utility. Third, the price-quality change shifts the participation cutoff θ*(q,p), so some consumers may enter or exit the market. The equilibrium consequence is that objective quality improvement can increase gross utility while decreasing ex ante consumer surplus for low or marginal types; the key results will identify the threshold conditions under which the direct quality effect dominates price pass-through and participation effects.

Where the novelty lies (select all that apply):
  [ ] New primitives (agents, action spaces, goods not in the ancestor)
  [ ] Modified information structure (who knows what, when)
  [ ] Different equilibrium concept (e.g., SPE instead of Nash)
  [x] New comparative statics direction or method
  [x] New welfare implication (different benchmark, planner comparison)
  [x] New policy interpretation (connects to quality upgrading rather than only product-line design)
  [ ] Extension to a broader class of environments (weakens an assumption)

This paper would NOT reduce to the ancestor model if:
  The model proves a quality-upgrade welfare decomposition and a firm-vs-planner quality distortion condition that cannot be obtained merely by renaming the canonical product-quality variables. In particular, the paper must retain the joint condition ΔCS = direct quality gain − price-pass-through loss ± participation/composition effect and use it to characterize type-specific gain/loss regions or private-vs-social quality wedges.
```
