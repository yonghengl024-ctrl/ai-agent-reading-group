## Structured Extraction

**Core claim or puzzle:**
Children of identical innate ability from urban and rural backgrounds may make different education investment choices due to China's hukou (household registration) system, which creates a dual urban-rural institutional structure. The puzzle is: if ability is held equal, what drives the divergence in optimal education investment?

**Economic phenomenon targeted:**
Educational investment disparities between urban and rural Chinese children of equal cognitive ability, specifically attributable to the hukou (户籍) registration system — an administrative institution that restricts rural-to-urban labor mobility and segments the returns to education across registration classes.

**Proposed mechanism (cause → channel → outcome):**
Hukou barrier (cause) → differential expected returns to human capital investment by registration status (channel) → equally-able urban and rural children face different marginal benefit schedules for education, yielding different optimal investment levels even when marginal cost schedules are identical (outcome). The framework proposed is Becker's human capital investment model.

**Agent types mentioned:**
Children (or their households as decision-making units) — one with urban hukou, one with rural hukou — both of equal innate ability.

**Environment or setting:**
Human capital investment setting (Becker framework): a single agent chooses how much education to acquire, weighing the marginal cost of investment against the marginal return. The researcher proposes extending this to two types of agents distinguished by hukou status, within China's dual urban-rural institutional structure.

**Mathematical intuition:**
Not explicitly specified. The Becker model implies a cost-benefit framework: invest until the marginal internal rate of return to human capital equals the opportunity cost of funds. With hukou barriers, the marginal benefit (return) curve likely shifts downward for rural children relative to equally-able urban children.

**Empirical or stylized motivation:**
China's urban-rural dual structure (城乡二元结构) and the hukou system are well-documented institutional features. The stylized fact motivating the puzzle is that rural children with the same measured ability as urban children tend to acquire less education — a pattern documented in Chinese development economics.

**Normative dimension (welfare / efficiency / policy):**
Implied but not explicitly stated. The hukou barrier creates an institutional wedge causing equally-able children to invest differently in human capital, which suggests a welfare loss relative to a frictionless benchmark. Policy implication: hukou reform could reduce education investment distortions.

**Comparable models or literature mentioned:**
- Becker's human capital investment model (explicitly named)
- China's urban-rural dual structure (城乡二元结构) — implicitly the Lewis dual-economy tradition

**Open questions explicitly stated:**
- Why would children of equal ability make different education investment choices when facing hukou barriers?
- (Implicit) How should the Becker model be modified to incorporate the dual structure and the hukou constraint?

**Ambiguities that may affect Stage 1:**
1. *Unit of decision-making*: Is the agent the child or the household? Becker's model allows both. This affects whether parental income or child ability is the binding constraint.
2. *Nature of the return differential*: Does the hukou barrier lower the wage level achievable with education, or does it lower the probability of accessing urban labor markets, or both? Each implies a different modelling choice.
3. *Single-period vs. multi-period*: The standard Becker model can be static (present value of lifetime earnings) or explicitly dynamic (two-period: invest then work). The researcher has not specified.
4. *Ability as private or public information*: Ability is held equal "by assumption," but whether this is observable to the labor market matters for how hukou interacts with signalling vs. pure investment.
5. *Scope of "different choices"*: Does this mean a discrete choice (enter higher education or not) or a continuous choice (years of schooling)? Both are defensible and yield different proposition structures.
